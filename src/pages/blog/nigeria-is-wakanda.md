Every time I say "Nigeria is Wakanda," somebody wants to laugh first.

And I understand why.

Too much around us looks like the opposite of Wakanda. Bad roads. Bad governance. Bad electricity. Bad incentives. Too much noise. Too much survival. Too much obvious proof that the country is underperforming itself.

But that is exactly why the sentence matters.

Wakanda was never really about gadgets. It was about a Black civilization with confidence in its own intelligence. A people that produced scientists, warriors, healers, storytellers, inventors, leaders, and institutions, and did not need permission from the outside world to believe in the quality of what it had produced.

That part should not sound strange to Nigerians.

Because Nigeria has already bred minds and spirits powerful enough to make the comparison feel less absurd than people think.

Our problem is not that this country cannot produce greatness. Our problem is that we keep producing greatness without building a culture mature enough to recognize it properly, study it seriously, and multiply it intentionally.

Nigeria is not empty of genius. Nigeria is forgetful of genius.

## The tragedy is not lack of talent

This country has given the world [Chinua Achebe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Chinua-Achebe), whose _Things Fall Apart_ did not merely become a famous novel. It helped restore African interiority to literature. Achebe refused to let Africa remain a backdrop in somebody else's story. He wrote our people as human beings with texture, dignity, intelligence, contradiction, humor, faith, failure, and moral weight.

That matters.

A nation is already richer than people think when it can produce a writer who changes how the world sees a civilization.

This country also gave the world [Wole Soyinka](https://www.nobelprize.org/laureate/663?from=NobelPress.org), who became the first Black African to win the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1986. But even that description is too small if you stop there. Soyinka matters not just because he is brilliant, but because he represents a kind of Nigerian seriousness we do not celebrate enough: intellectual power joined to moral courage. He wrote, he confronted power, he paid prices for saying what he believed, and he refused to become decorative.

That too is part of nation-building.

We have [Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Funmilayo-Ransome-Kuti), who organized women politically, confronted unjust taxation, and helped expand the idea of what public courage could look like in Nigeria. She did not wait for history to become favorable. She pushed history.

We have [Dora Akunyili](https://www.wipo.int/web/wipo-magazine/articles/joining-forces-to-combat-counterfeiting-35082), who took on the counterfeit drug trade with a level of courage many public officials never come close to. She did not just hold office. She made office mean something. In a country where public institutions often feel like theaters of compromise, she turned NAFDAC into a place that actually frightened criminals.

We have [Dr. Stella Adadevoh](https://www.cnn.com/2016/09/15/africa/93-days-nigerian-film-ebola-heroes), whose refusal to let Patrick Sawyer walk out of a hospital in Lagos during the 2014 Ebola crisis helped prevent a catastrophe in one of the largest cities on earth. That was not abstract patriotism. That was professional duty at its highest form. Competence can save a country. One serious person in one room can save millions of people who will never know their names.

We have [Ngozi Okonjo-Iweala](https://www.wto.org/English/thewto_e/dg_e/dg_e.htm), who became the first woman and the first African to lead the World Trade Organization, and whose career has become one of the clearest Nigerian examples of technical competence at global scale. But even before Geneva, she had already shown what serious public service can look like when a mind is trained, disciplined, and stubborn enough to confront institutional rot.

And yes, [Prof. Ndubuisi Ekekwe](https://www.tekedia.com/lead-faculty-of-tekedia-institute/) belongs in this conversation too. What I find valuable in his example is not merely pedigree. It is synthesis. Semiconductor work. Patents. Teaching. Writing. Investing. Institution-building through Tekedia. The point is not that every Nigerian must become an engineer. The point is that a Nigerian mind can operate at global technical depth and still choose to build intellectual infrastructure for Africans instead of disappearing into private success.

That is the sort of pattern a country should study.

And even with Philip Emeagwali, we should learn an important lesson in precision. Strip away the mythology and the exaggerated folklore, and one real thing still remains: he did win the 1989 Gordon Bell Prize for supercomputing work on oil-reservoir modeling. A serious country should not need to invent fake crowns for its people. The real achievements are already enough. Truthful honor is stronger than propaganda.

That last point matters more than people think.

Because part of what has damaged Nigeria is our habit of replacing disciplined admiration with empty hype. We do not know how to celebrate people honestly. We either ignore them while they are doing real work, or exaggerate them into cartoons and then act surprised when the story collapses under scrutiny.

A mature nation must learn to praise with accuracy.

## A country becomes what it rewards

The deeper issue is not whether Nigeria has produced great people. It obviously has.

The deeper issue is who occupies our imagination now.

Who do our children hear about most often?

Who gets the loudest applause?

Who gets turned into aspiration?

Too often, it is the loudest politician, the flashiest fraudster, the emptiest celebrity, the nearest symbol of money without mastery, influence without contribution, fame without discipline.

And then we wonder why the national mind starts bending in ugly directions.

You do not correct the brain of a country only by changing presidents. You correct it by changing who the people secretly worship.

If the average young Nigerian grows up admiring noise, they will imitate noise.

If they grow up admiring shortcuts, they will imitate shortcuts.

If they grow up admiring stolen wealth, they will normalize theft.

If they grow up admiring people who have built something serious, thought something serious, written something serious, defended something serious, or sacrificed for something serious, that admiration starts reorganizing the imagination.

That is why role models are not soft culture talk. They are national infrastructure.

Heroes are curriculum.

What a people clap for long enough becomes what more of them try to become.

## We have not built enough memory around our best people

One of the reasons I keep saying "Nigeria is Wakanda" is because I am tired of the lie that our deficiency is genetic, civilizational, or intellectual.

It is not.

Our deficiency is institutional, cultural, and moral.

We have not built enough schools, films, stories, scholarships, research labs, and ordinary household memory around the Nigerians who should be shaping the country's aspiration.

That is partly why I wrote [Nollywood Is Starving the Nigerian Imagination](/blog/nollywood-is-starving-the-nigerian-imagination). A nation this full of dramatic lives, intellectual giants, reformers, scientists, activists, and civilizational raw material should not feel this narratively poor.

Why are there not more mainstream films about Dora Akunyili's war against counterfeit drugs?

Why are there not more children's books that make Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti feel as familiar to a 10-year-old as foreign cartoon characters?

Why are there not more beautifully made series about the making of Achebe, Soyinka, and the minds that shaped Nigeria's literary dignity?

Why do we know so much gossip about the famous and so little discipline about the admirable?

That is not a small cultural defect. That is a serious national weakness.

A country with weak memory cannot transmit standards properly.

And when standards are not transmitted, mediocrity stops feeling embarrassing.

## Nigeria's real vibranium is not in the ground

Let me put the Wakanda metaphor plainly.

Nigeria's real vibranium is not crude oil. It is not lithium. It is not land. It is not population size by itself.

It is people.

It is the frightening amount of intelligence this country keeps producing under conditions that should have crushed far more of it.

It is the child in Aba, Enugu, Kano, Lagos, Jos, Ibadan, Port Harcourt, Abeokuta, Maiduguri, or Owerri who is smarter than the opportunities around them.

It is the girl who could become a world-class scientist if she meets one serious teacher.

It is the boy who could become a great novelist if somebody puts the right books in his hands early enough.

It is the young doctor who could become another Stella Adadevoh.

It is the young public servant who could become another Dora Akunyili.

It is the young economist who could become another Okonjo-Iweala.

It is the young thinker who could become another Achebe or Soyinka, if only we stop feeding them a culture that treats thought as decorative and seriousness as uncool.

That is the heartbreak of Nigeria. The raw material is scandalously rich. The conversion system is weak.

We have children with first-class minds being raised inside third-class incentives.

We have genius without enough guidance, courage without enough structure, brilliance without enough institutions, and ambition without enough canon.

That is why the country feels like a permanent near-miss.

## What should we actually emulate?

Not just success.

That word has become too cheap.

We should emulate Achebe's clarity.

We should emulate Soyinka's fearlessness.

We should emulate Funmilayo's organizing instinct.

We should emulate Dora's incorruptible public courage.

We should emulate Stella's professional duty.

We should emulate Okonjo-Iweala's competence.

We should emulate Ndubuisi Ekekwe's commitment to building intellectual and business infrastructure from African soil instead of merely exporting talent.

And even in the Philip Emeagwali story, told carefully and honestly, we should emulate the hunger for difficult work while rejecting the temptation to inflate reality. A people serious about greatness must also be serious about truth.

That is what I want Nigerians to recover: not shallow patriotism, not empty chest-beating, not "we are the giants of Africa" slogans repeated by people doing unserious work.

I want us to recover a patriotism of standards.

A patriotism that says this country has produced too many serious people for us to keep acting unseriously.

A patriotism that says our children should know these names.

A patriotism that says a nation is partly repaired when it learns to admire the right things again.

## Nigeria is Wakanda, but only if we act like it

So no, I do not mean Nigeria is Wakanda because we already look like an advanced utopia.

Obviously we do not.

I mean Nigeria is Wakanda in potential, in raw human capital, in civilizational possibility, in the evidence already sitting inside our history, literature, medicine, public service, engineering, activism, and scholarship.

I mean the country has already shown you enough proof.

The question is whether we will finally build a culture that deserves the people it keeps producing.

Will we keep feeding the youth a diet of noise, fraud, and shallow aspiration?

Or will we start putting better names in their mouths?

Will we keep acting as if the highest dream is to be rich and seen?

Or will we remind them that the country has produced men and women whose greatness was deeper than attention?

Nigeria will not become sane merely because we denounce what is wrong.

Nigeria becomes saner when we also enthrone what is worthy.

That is how you correct the brain of a country.

You give it better saints.

You give it better models.

You give it better memory.

And then you tell the truth, again and again, until the next generation starts believing a more demanding story about what a Nigerian can be.

Nigeria is Wakanda.

Not because of fantasy.

Because of what this country has already, repeatedly, painfully, gloriously bred.
